A Brief History of the Texas Top 10 Percent Rule

  • 1996 - The U.S. 5th Circuit Court of Appeals rules that UT-Austin's law school discriminated against white applicants, effectively ending the use of affirmative action in Texas.
  • 1997 - The Texas Legislature creates the Top 10 Percent Rule, promising students who graduate at the top of their class automatic admission to the state's public universities.
  • 2003 - The U.S. Supreme Court upholds the use of affirmative action. UT-Austin begins to again consider race when evaluating non-top 10 percent applicants.
  • 2008 - Abigail Fisher, a white student from Sugar Land, is denied admission to UT-Austin and later sues the university claiming that she was penalized because of her race.
  • 2009 - The Texas Legislature tweaks the Top 10 Percent Rule, allowing UT-Austin to cap the number of automatically admitted freshmen at 75 percent of the overall class.
  • 2012 - Fisher's lawsuit reaches the U.S. Supreme Court.
  • 2013 - The Supreme Court issues a limited ruling on Fisher's lawsuit, effectively upholding affirmative action.
  • 2015 - The U.S. Supreme Court agrees to re-hear the Fisher case.
